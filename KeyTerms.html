Territoriality -- To read territory is to read a degree of power over space, and certainly their relationship is inextricable. Our behaviors and orientations are daily influenced by material-territoriality; parking lots, buildings, street signs constrict our spatial movements and monopolize a degree of visual public attention. The relationship between human territoriality and urban public space has opened insights into economies of visibility and public attention, as well as the territory-making capacity of certain groups to participate in spatial forms of public discourse. In Brighenti's (2010) study of graffiti writers and urban territoriality, she moves away from static notions of territorial space and power to seeing territoriality as a process of ongoing negotiations between various stakeholders. This naturally resists primordialist and strategist claims that certain groups are "'naturally' attached to some place or region--such as the 'Fatherland'" or as in strategism, insisting territoriality only refers to establishing control over a given space (p. 324). Brighenti's view of territoriality allows for the concept to be a study of ongoing negotiations of place-meaning and practice that are marked by specific communities and materials. Territoriality is then "deeply social in the sense of something that is coessential to the innner and outer relationships within multiplicity of socii" (p.324).
